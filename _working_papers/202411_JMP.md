Abstract: Return policy design is central to social welfare, yet its welfare effects are ambiguous when policies jointly shape purchases, prices, and return behavior. Policy effects on social welfare operate through both return discipline and market participation, and the two margins can pull in opposite directions. Strengthening return discipline alone need not raise social welfare: when return rates fall, sellers may raise equilibrium prices and exclude socially efficient purchases. To evaluate alternative return policies, we estimate a structural model of purchase and return decisions using transaction data from a women's clothing retailer on Taobao that repeatedly switches between retailers' return shipping insurance (RRSI) and consumers' return shipping insurance (CRSI). We also simulate return-penalty discount (RPD), in which buyers choose full refunds at list price or a discounted price with stricter refunds. RRSI attains the highest social welfare in dress, where high markups make participation gains outweigh over-return. In other categories, full RPD outperforms both RSI regimes by strengthening return discipline. Partial RPD with restocking fees of 5-20% preserves much of that return discipline while expanding participation relative to full forfeiture, outperforming both RSI regimes in most categories and nearly matching RRSI in dress. Return policy should be tailored to category margins and return-handling costs rather than imposed uniformly.
